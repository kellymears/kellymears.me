I didn't start with software. I started with sound.

My undergrad thesis at The Bard College at Simon's Rock explored Japanese soundscapes — how ambient noise shapes place and meaning in a culture that listens differently. That training in pattern recognition and systems thinking turned out to be exactly the foundation I needed for everything that came after.

The web pulled me in because it was the most democratic infrastructure I'd ever encountered. I landed in the nonprofit and advocacy space, spending years at **Other98** building the digital backbone for national grassroots campaigns — from Standing Rock to Fight for $15. That work taught me that technology isn't neutral: the tools you build and who you build them for are deeply political choices.

From there I founded **Tiny Pixel Collective**, a consulting studio focused on progressive organizations. I built platforms for groups like **NDN Collective** and dozens of advocacy orgs, always with the same question in mind: how do you make powerful tools accessible to people who are too busy changing the world to fight with their CMS?

That question led me to open source. I became the primary author of **bud.js**, the build system for the Roots WordPress ecosystem. Over four years I shipped 30+ packages, wrote 854+ pull requests, and built tooling that's now downloaded ~13,000 times a week. The project earned an Arctic Code Vault contribution badge, which is one of those things that sounds made up but is actually very cool.

These days I'm an engineering specialist at **Carrot**, building scalable web applications for a benefits platform that serves over 8,000 customers. I still maintain open source work, still think a lot about developer experience, and I've gotten very interested in how AI is changing the way we write and think about code.

When I'm not at a keyboard, I'm cycling around Winston-Salem, NC, or being supervised by my cats. The cats have strong opinions about everything, especially food, and they are not shy about sharing them.
